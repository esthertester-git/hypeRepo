Element 1 of 10

Element 1: The Problem

You have already done this work. Your problem statement from Module 1 is Element 1.

When you are pitching, this is where you start. Lead with the customer's pain, make it specific, and include the consequence. Do not rush it.

If you have not completed Module 1, go back and complete the M1 Core Story Template in your Pitching Workbook before continuing. Everything that follows builds on it.

Element 2: Your Solution

Your UVP from Module 1 is the core of Element 2.

In a full pitch, you will expand it slightly to make clear what you actually do and why it works. Your solution should directly answer the problem you just described.

When you move from the problem to your solution, your audience should immediately see the connection and feel that your solution is the logical response. If there is a gap between the pain you described and the product you are presenting, that is worth fixing now.

Keep your solution description outcome-focused. Remember: You are not describing features or technology. You are describing what changes for your customer.

Element 3: Market Opportunity

The silent question your audience is asking here:

Is this worth pursuing?

Is the market big enough?

Your audience wants to know there is a real, sizable opportunity: not just for your business, but for the market you are entering. Frame your market size in three layers (TAM, SAM, and SOM) and be ready to explain why now is the right moment for your solution. Your Pitch Content Worksheet will guide you through both.

Element 4: Business Model

The silent question your audience is asking here:

How do you make money?

Your business model does not have to be complicated, but it does have to be clear. Explain how you generate revenue, how you price your offering, and whether that pricing is validated. If you have unit economics (your customer acquisition cost and lifetime value), include them. Your Pitch Content Worksheet will guide you through each piece.

Element 5: Go-to-Market Strategy

TThe silent question your audience is asking here:

How are you going to reach your customers?

A great product with no path to market is still a business that will struggle. Your go-to-market strategy explains who you are targeting first, how you will reach them, and how your early customers will help you attract more. Be specific about who your early customers are: what industry or sector they are in, what size of organisation or type of individual they represent, and what characteristic makes them the right first audience for your solution. Keep it grounded and specific.

Element 6: Competitive Landscape

The silent question your audience is asking here:

Why you, and not someone else?

Do not skip this section. Acknowledging competition shows you understand the market. Pretending you have no competitors suggests you have not done your research. Cover who else is solving this problem, what makes you genuinely different, and why that difference matters to your customer.

Element 7: Your Team

The silent question your audience is asking here:

Can this team actually do this?

Your audience is often as focused on the team as they are on the idea. A strong team with a flawed idea can recognise the problem and change direction. A weak team with a strong idea may not be able to bring it to life successfully. Cover who is building this, what relevant expertise you bring, and any gaps you are actively working to fill. You do not need to be the world's foremost expert on this problem. What your audience wants to know is why you are motivated to solve it, what you bring to it, and that you are self-aware enough to know what you still need to learn or hire.

Element 8: Traction and Milestones

The silent question your audience is asking here:

Is this working?

What evidence do you have?

Traction is any evidence that your idea is connecting with the real world. It reduces the risk in your pitch because it shows you are not just planning; you are taking action and learning as you go. Be honest about where you are, and include your key upcoming milestones. If your traction feels thin, do not discount it. Early-stage evidence counts: customer conversations, a pilot, a letter of intent, or even consistent feedback confirming the problem is real. What your audience wants to see is that you are moving and learning, not that you have already figured everything out.

Element 9: Financial Projections

The silent question your audience is asking here:

What does the future look like,
and is it believable?

Financial projections are not a test of your ability to predict the future. They are a test of your thinking: your assumptions about growth, costs, and the key factors that drive your business forward. This element is most relevant when pitching to investors. The most important thing is that your projections are defensible.

Element 10: The Ask

The silent question your audience is asking here:

What do you need from me,
and what will you do with it?

This is the element many founders rush or leave vague. Do not. Your ask should be specific, clear, and tied directly to what you want your audience to do next. It will look different depending on whether you are pitching to a customer, a partner, or an investor. A vague ask signals that you have not thought through what you actually need.
